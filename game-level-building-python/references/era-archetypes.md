# Era Archetypes

Use this as a safe style guide when the user asks for ancient Chinese layouts spanning Shang, Zhou, Han, or Three Kingdoms inspirations.

## Practical Style Assumption

Treat these requests as a broad ancient-to-classical Chinese palette, not a single exact dynasty reconstruction.

Good recurring motifs:

- rammed-earth terraces and precinct walls
- axial gate-to-court-to-hall progression
- raised halls on plinths
- watchtowers and gate towers
- mirrored side halls or service wings
- warehouses, barracks courts, and drill yards
- shrine terraces and summit compounds
- simple formal garden courts with ponds, trees, pavilions, and straight paths
- ward blocks, alley courts, and market-side residential compounds
- tomb mounds, spirit paths, flanking markers, and walled burial courts

Avoid using this skill to imply:

- museum-grade historical reconstruction
- highly ornate later scholar-garden detailing by default
- fully accurate timber joinery or dynasty-specific ornament unless the user explicitly asks for that level of fidelity

## Safe Template Families

For Shang and Zhou flavored layouts, lean toward:

- walled precincts
- rammed-earth platforms
- ceremonial terraces
- simple gate and hall sequences

For Han and Three Kingdoms flavored layouts, lean toward:

- axial compounds
- courtyard halls
- multistory watchtowers
- fortified gates
- service yards, granaries, and military-adjacent precincts
- dense ward blocks with narrow circulation lanes
- funerary precincts with axial approaches, marker courts, and raised tomb masses

## Garden Guidance

Keep garden templates restrained and structural:

- rectangular ponds
- axial paths
- pavilion anchors
- tree courts
- low walls
- bridges or stepping approaches only when they support the main circulation

Prefer compound courts and ritualized open space over later highly ornamental rock gardens unless the user explicitly wants a cross-era blend.

## Funerary Guidance

Keep tomb and mausoleum layouts bold and ceremonial:

- raised mound or chamber mass as the dominant landmark
- axial spirit path or processional approach
- flanking pylons, markers, or guardian platforms
- perimeter wall or burial court enclosure
- secondary ritual halls or offering pavilions kept simpler than the central tomb mass

Avoid defaulting to highly ornate late-imperial tomb decoration unless the user explicitly asks for a later historical blend.
